5.1. Terms and definitions

The number of days each month has is defined in following table:

Table 3.16 Calendar Month
MM Calendar Month Number of Calendar Days
01  January 31
02 February, common year 28
02 February, leap year 29
03 March 31
04 April 30
05 May 31
06 June 30
07 July 31
08 August 31
09 September 30
10 October 31
11 November 30
12 December 31

Leap years are years with an additional day YYYY-02-29, where the year number is a multiple of four with the following exception: if a year is a multiple of 100, then it is only a leap year if it is also a multiple of 400. For example, 1900 was not a leap year, but 2000 is one.

Leap seconds cannot be predicted far into the future. The International Earth Rotation Service publishes bulletins [IERS] that announce leap seconds with a few weeks' warning. Applications should not generate timestamps involving inserted leap seconds until after the leap seconds are announced.

All date and time formats are represented with the largest units given first, from right to left.
A missing part of the date or time should be replaced with the hyphen character (-) to avoid ambiguity.
Unless otherwise stated, all values are fixed width, with leading zeros used when necessary to pad out a value.
Some formats require alphabetic letters, which should be upper case, although lower case may be used if upper case is not available.

5.2 Dates

The standard specifies only numeric notations and does not cover dates and times where words are used in the representation. A calendar date is identified by a given day in a given month in a given year. An ordinal date is identified by a given day in a given year.
Dates can be given in year-month-day, year-week-day or year-day formats.

Table 5.2.1 Partial list of calendar date representations
Format Example
Basic format YYYYMMDD 20030114
Extended format YYYY-MM-DD 2003-01-14
Reduced precision
 Month basic format YYYY-MM 2003-01
Year basic format YYYY 2003
Century basic format YY 20
Ordinal date
Basic format YYYYddd 2003102
Extended format YYYY-ddd 2003-102
Week date
Basic format YYYYWwwn 2003W012
Extended format YYYY-Www-n 2003-W01-2
Basic reduced format YYYYWww 2003W01
Extended reduced format YYYY-Www 2003-W01
  • YYYY is the year in the Gregorian Calendar; generally represented by four digits in the range [0000, 9999].
    Meaning of the numbers from [0000, 1582] depends of the implementation.
  • MM is the month of the year between January and December; represented by two digits in the range [01, 12].
  • DD is the day of the month; represented by two digits in the range [01, 31].
  • ddd is the day of the year; represented by three digits in the range [001, 366]. Leap years have 366 days.
  • ww is the ordinal number of a calendar week within the year;  represented by two digits in the range [01, 53].
    Week 01 of a year is per definition the first week that has the Thursday in this year. If a week contain days from the previous year and the days of the current year it is in same time Week 01 of the current year and Week 52 or 53 of the previous year. Any given year has at least 52 weeks. Exception is the year 1582. having only 355 days and therefore less than 52 weeks.
  • n is a day of the week;  represented by one digit in the range [1, 7].  The day 1 is Monday and the week ends with Sunday which is day 7.

5.3 Time of the Day

The standard specifies only time notation and it is based on 24-hour clock.

Table 5.3 Partial list of the time of the day representations
Format Example
Basic format hhmmss 235959
Extended format hh:mm:ss 23:59:59
Reduced precision
Minute basic format hhmm 2359
Minute extended format hh:mm 23:59
Hour basic format hh 23
Decimal fractions
Basic format hhmmss, ffff 2359,9942
Extended format hh:mm:ss, ffff 23:59,9942
Hour reduced format hh,ffff 23,50
Time designator
Basic format YYYYMMDDT hhmmss 20030114T235959
Extended format YYYY-MM-DDT hh:mm:ss 2003-01-14T23:59:59
  • hh is the number of complete hours that have passed since midnight; represented by two digits in the range [00, 24].
    The Hour 00 is the beginning of any given day and the Hour 24 is the end of the day. Midnight will normally be represented as 0000 or 2400. Hour 24  have the minute and second values zero 24:00:00.
  • mm is the number of complete minutes that have passed since the start of the hour; represented by two digits in the range [00, 59].
  • ss is the number of complete seconds since the start of the minute; represented by two digits in the range [00, 60]. Value 60 allows to add a leap second to the Coordinated Universal Time (UTC).
  • ffff is the decimal fraction of the preceding time unit (hour, minute or second); it is represented by any previously agreed number of digits bigger or equal to one. Decimal sign is comma (,)  or dot (.), but comma is preferred.
  • Inserting time designator T is preferred way to store date and time in a single field.

5.3.4.1Time Zones

Table 5.3.4 Partial list of the time zone representations
Format Example
Coordinated Universal Time UTC
 Minute basic format hhmmssZ 235959Z
Minute extended format hh:mm:ssZ 23:59:59Z
Local time
Basic format hhmmss+ HHNN 235959-0500
Extended format hh:mm:ss+ HH:NN 23:59:59-05:00
Hour reduced format hh:mm:ss+ HH 23:59:59-05
  • HH is the time zone offset in hours;
  • NN is the time zone offset in minutes;
  • Coordinated Universal Time represented as time followed with letter Z.
  • Sign before time zone value is + if the local time presented is ahead of equal UTC time; if local time is behind of same UTC time, the sign is -.
  • Time zone could be represented only with hours if the minute portion of the difference between the local time and UTC is zero.
